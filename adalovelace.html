Who was Ada?

Ada Lovelace was born in 1815. She was the only child of a volatile poet father and her mother, Annabella, who loved math. Annabella encouraged Ada to learn about math, science, and logic. She noticed Ada's love of mechanics and talent for math from an early age.

At 19, Ada married William King, Earl of Lovelace. When she was around 20, Ada's mentor, Mary Sommerville, introduced her to Charles Babbage. Babbage was a professor who had an unfinished plan for a calculator machine. He was considered "strange" just like Ada so they were very good friends. Tey admired each other. Charles called her "The Enchantress of Numbers."

Babbage and Lovelace worked together on making his plan a reality. He asked her to translate an article from an Italian mathematician. He told her to add comments. Her comments ended up being 3 times as long as the article. She included many ideas for the use of the machine, including making music, and codes to be used. It was published in England as "Sketch of the Analytical Engine, With Notes from the Translator."

Ada died of cancer a couple years after this publication. She was only 36. However, her writings were used as the framework for the inventor of the first modern computer, Alan Turing, in the 1940s.

How is Ada's life still affecting us today?

Without Ada's writings and passion for computing, we would not have the amazing technologies we have today. These include:

  • computers
  • calculators
  • cell phones
  • AND MANY MORE!

Ada also inspired many of the most famous computer programmers and inventors of the modern world. Including Alan Turing who invented the first modern computer. Ada Lovelace continues to inspire the next generation of women in science and mathematics on "Ada Lovelace Day." This is a celebration of the contributions of women in the technology feild as well as an oppurtunity to thank the women in your life who have inspired you to pursue a career in IT.
